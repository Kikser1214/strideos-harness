# Training method and progression reference

Use this reference to build a baseline, research a named method, or adapt load.

## Planning hierarchy

Resolve constraints in this order:

1. current safety and pain;
2. recent tolerable training and confirmed recovery;
3. realistic schedule and environment;
4. goal demands and deadline;
5. athlete preference;
6. named training style.

A method never outranks safety, history, or recovery.

## Stage defaults

### Starter

- three separated run-walk-run sessions;
- two short technique-first strength sessions;
- optional easy cycling only when it replaces or supports appropriate aerobic work;
- progress duration before intensity;
- no advanced threshold density.

### Returning

- rebuild consistent easy frequency below the prior peak;
- preserve walk breaks or cross-training where helpful;
- reintroduce faster work only after current tolerance is clear;
- avoid using old race fitness as current capacity.

### Building

- maintain an easy-volume majority;
- add one clear quality purpose at a time;
- protect strength, recovery, and the long run;
- monitor whether the athlete is absorbing the work, not merely completing it.

### Established

- preserve the athlete's demonstrated volume and recovery rhythm;
- target the limiting demand: aerobic support, threshold, economy, speed, durability, hills, or race specificity;
- change one major load variable at a time;
- use subjective report alongside device data.

## Method research template

For every named approach, record:

- exact name and primary or authoritative source;
- population and goal it was designed for;
- session pattern and weekly load distribution;
- intensity definition and measurement method;
- prerequisites and recovery demand;
- evidence quality and limitations;
- unsafe or misleading popular simplifications;
- athlete-specific fit;
- full use, conservative adaptation, or rejection.

Do not treat “Norwegian” as automatically meaning double threshold. Do not describe “African training” as a single system. Research the specific coach, group, protocol, or study the athlete means.

## Session specification

Every planned session should state:

- type and purpose;
- duration or distance;
- effort or pace range and how to judge it;
- warm-up, recoveries, and cool-down where relevant;
- terrain or equipment;
- completion and stop rules;
- easier alternative when conditions change.

Strength sessions should identify movement patterns, sets, repetitions or time, technique priority, equipment substitutions, and progression boundary. Avoid false precision when the athlete's technique or load tolerance is unknown.

## Load and adaptation rules

- Never double the next day or stack intensity to catch up a missed session.
- Two or more missed sessions normally mean repeat or simplify the week.
- New pain, illness, unusual fatigue, or a safety concern pauses progression.
- Poor recovery may move, shorten, or replace quality work; it does not automatically erase all movement.
- Heat, hills, trail, altitude, and wind can make pace a poor effort proxy.
- A recovery week reduces load before the next block when appropriate.
- Confirm completion rather than inferring it from a similar imported activity.

## Weekly review

Compare separately:

- planned sessions;
- observed activities;
- athlete-confirmed completion;
- effort and session feel;
- pain and symptoms;
- sleep, stress, and life constraints;
- missing or stale evidence.

Conclude with one of: repeat, simplify, progress, or reassess. Explain the evidence and wait for plan approval.
